## Words and phrases

**pipeline** — A series of connected steps that move something from a start to a result.

**conceptualization** — Deciding the idea or message you want to express.

**formulation** — Turning an idea into words, phrases, and sentences.

**articulation** — Moving the mouth and speech organs to produce sounds.

**retrieval** — Finding known information in memory when you need it.

**follow-up question** — A new question based on something a person just said.

**transfer** — Using a skill or piece of language in a different task or situation.

**corrective feedback** — Information that helps a learner notice and improve an error.

**recast** — A correction that repeats a learner’s idea in the correct form.

**prompt** — A word, question, or signal that asks the learner to produce the correction.

**intelligibility** — How easily another person can understand your speech.

**repair** — Fixing or explaining a message after something becomes unclear.

**correlation** — A relationship that does not by itself prove cause.

**freeze** — Suddenly become unable to think or speak.

**get the words out** — Manage to say what you are trying to say.

**make a point** — Express one clear idea or opinion.

**buy time** — Use a short phrase to gain a moment for thinking.

**rephrase** — Express the same meaning in a different way.

**get stuck** — Become unable to continue for a moment.

**make yourself understood** — Communicate clearly enough for another person to understand.

**keep the meaning alive** — Continue communicating even when a word or sentence fails.

## 2. Speaking Is a Pipeline

**Nora:** Eli, Amir says he knows English. Is he wrong?

**Eli:** Not necessarily. Knowing a word and finding it quickly during speech are different abilities. Speaking happens through a pipeline.

**Nora:** A pipeline is a series of connected steps.

**Eli:** First, you decide what you want to say. Researchers call this conceptualization. We can simply call it the idea stage.

**Nora:** For Amir’s city question, the idea stage might be: friendly people, good food, and a beautiful river.

**Eli:** Second, you turn the idea into language. You find words, choose grammar, and build phrases. This is the formulation stage.

**Nora:** Third, your mouth produces the sounds. That is articulation.

**Eli:** And while all this happens, you monitor your message. You notice a mistake, check the listener’s face, change a word, or explain something again.

**Nora:** Real conversation adds another demand. You have to listen, take your turn, answer follow-up questions, and sometimes repair a misunderstanding.

**Eli:** So Amir’s speech can break down in several places. Maybe he cannot choose a clear point. Maybe he knows the words but cannot retrieve them quickly. Maybe he can build the sentence but worries about every grammar choice. Maybe the sounds feel difficult. Or maybe he can give a prepared answer but cannot react to a second question.

**Nora:** This explains something important. Two learners may both say, “I cannot speak,” but they may need completely different practice.

**Eli:** Exactly. One learner needs more useful language. Another needs faster retrieval. Another needs pronunciation feedback. Another needs real interaction.

**Nora:** A study of one hundred and twenty-eight learners examined different parts of oral fluency. It found a complex picture, but processing speed was connected with several parts of fluent speech across different tasks.

**Eli:** In simple English, language in your notebook is not enough. The language must become available while the clock is moving.

**Nora:** Then the first question is not, “How bad is my speaking?”

**Eli:** It is, “Where does my speaking stop?”

**Nora:** Let’s give Amir a quick diagnosis. Imagine you ask him a question in his first language: “What do you like about your city?” If he still cannot choose a point, the problem begins with ideas and organization.

**Nora:** If the keywords are easy but the sentence takes a long time, retrieval and formulation may need practice.

**Eli:** If the sentence is clear but hard to understand, work on a small pronunciation target.

**Nora:** And if his first answer is fine but the second question destroys him—

## 3. What Popular Advice Gets Right—and What It Misses

**Nora:** Let’s test some common advice. Number one: “Speak more.”

**Eli:** Correct, but incomplete. Watching a video about push-ups does not train your arms. Watching a video about speaking does not give you speaking practice unless you produce language.

**Nora:** Number two: “Think in English.”

**Eli:** Useful when it means starting with simple English ideas instead of translating a perfect paragraph. Name things. Describe what you are doing. Make a short choice: “I’ll take the train because it is cheaper.”

**Nora:** But learners do not need to treat their first language like an enemy.

**Eli:** Right. You cannot press a button and remove it. The practical goal is to build faster paths from meaning to English.

**Nora:** Number three: “Shadow native speakers.” Shadowing means repeating very close behind a recording.

**Eli:** It can help you notice stress, rhythm, linking, and pronunciation. But if all your practice is copying, you are training your mouth to follow another person’s message. You are not fully training your brain to create your own.

**Nora:** So imitate one short line, then close the text and answer a new question with the same sound pattern or phrase.

**Eli:** Exactly. Number four: “Learn phrases, not only words.”

**Nora:** This one has some research support. Useful word groups can reduce the work of building every sentence one word at a time.

**Eli:** Instead of learning only the word “point,” learn “The main point is…” Instead of only “example,” learn “A good example would be…” Instead of only “disagree,” learn “I see it differently because…”

**Nora:** But a list of one thousand “native phrases” is not a speaking system.

**Eli:** No. Choose three to five phrases for a real purpose, then use them in several original answers.

**Nora:** Number five: “Become confident first.”

**Eli:** That advice sounds kind, but it can keep learners waiting. Confidence often grows after small successful experiences. You do not need to feel ready before you record thirty seconds.

**Nora:** And finally, “Speak fast to sound fluent.”

**Eli:** Please do not race the language. Fluency is not only speed. It also includes pauses, repairs, and how easily the listener can follow the message. A natural pause at the end of an idea can help. A long pause in the middle of every small phrase may show that retrieval is difficult.

**Nora:** The goal is not to remove every pause.

**Eli:** The goal is to make yourself understood without using all your energy to build each sentence.

## 5. Why Repetition and Feedback Work Together

**Eli:** Research gives us a useful reason for the improvement we just heard. When you repeat the same speaking task, you already know the message. That reduces the mental work of planning the ideas.

**Nora:** It also makes useful words and sentence patterns easier to find the second time.

**Eli:** In one classroom experiment, twenty-four students gave speeches in four, three, and two minutes. Some repeated the same topic. Others changed topics. Both groups improved during practice, but the group that repeated the same speech maintained the fluency improvement on later tests.

**Nora:** Twenty-four is a small group, so we should not turn one study into a law.

**Eli:** Right. But it supports a practical tool: say the same message again with less time.

**Nora:** Newer research adds another detail. Immediate repetition can improve the next performance, but spaced practice and changes in the task may help different parts of fluency and transfer.

**Eli:** That is why our plan uses two kinds of repetition. First, repeat the same answer now. Later, answer a related question or repeat the procedure on another day.

**Nora:** What about feedback? Many learners either receive none, or they ask someone to correct everything.

**Eli:** A meta-analysis combined fifteen classroom studies with eight hundred and twenty-seven learners. It found that oral corrective feedback had meaningful and lasting effects on language development. Prompts were stronger than simple recasts in that analysis.

**Nora:** Define those two words.

**Eli:** A recast gives the correct form. Learner: “He go yesterday.” Teacher: “Yes, he went yesterday.” A prompt asks the learner to produce the change. Teacher: “He…?” Learner: “He went yesterday.”

**Nora:** The prompt makes the learner retrieve the improved form.

**Eli:** Yes. Another study followed one hundred and sixty-seven university learners for a semester. Groups trained to use corrective feedback during peer interaction improved in both accuracy and fluency. A group that interacted without that feedback improved over the control group on fluency, but not in the same way on accuracy.

**Nora:** So conversation and correction can do different jobs.

**Eli:** And the timing matters in practice. During a fluency round, let the learner finish the point. After the answer, choose one or two recurring problems. Give a prompt, let the learner repair the sentence, and then repeat the message.

**Nora:** If I interrupt Amir after every small error, what happens?

**Eli:** His monitoring system may take control of the whole conversation. He begins checking every word before it leaves his mouth.

**Nora:** Which makes the speaking problem worse.

**Eli:** Often, yes. We need an accuracy moment and a fluency moment. They can support each other without fighting for the same second.

## 6. Chunks, Pronunciation, and Real Interaction

**Nora:** Let’s build Amir a small speaking kit. Not one thousand phrases. Five.

**Eli:** For giving an opinion: “From my point of view…”

**Nora:** For organizing: “The main point is…”

**Eli:** For adding: “Another thing to consider is…”

**Nora:** For buying time: “Let me think for a second.”

**Eli:** And for repair: “What I mean is…”

**Nora:** These are chunks—groups of words that often work together.

**Eli:** A five-week study compared learners who practiced eighty multiword sequences with learners who studied single academic words. The phrase group improved more on one measure of speech rate, but not on every measure of pauses and repairs.

**Nora:** An earlier small study also found that learners trained to notice formulaic sequences were judged as more proficient in an interview.

**Eli:** Useful evidence, but limited evidence. The safe conclusion is that reusable phrases can help formulation. They do not remove the need for ideas, grammar, pronunciation, and interaction.

**Nora:** How should Amir learn the five phrases?

**Eli:** First, hear them in a real example. Second, notice their meaning and rhythm. Third, use each one in a new answer. Fourth, return to them later without looking.

**Nora:** Now pronunciation. Should he try to remove his accent?

**Eli:** His first goal is intelligibility: can the listener understand him without unreasonable effort? Accent and intelligibility are not the same thing.

**Nora:** A large review of pronunciation instruction included eighty-six reports. Overall, instruction helped. Longer training, feedback, and controlled practice often showed larger effects.

**Eli:** But the controlled-practice result is also a warning. Saying one practiced sentence clearly does not automatically create clear spontaneous speech.

**Nora:** So Amir chooses one target from his recording. Maybe the stress in “public transportation,” or the ending of a past-tense verb.

**Eli:** He listens to a short model, copies it a few times, and then uses it in his own answer. This is where imitation or shadowing fits.

**Nora:** Small tool, clear job.

**Eli:** Exactly. Now the final part: interaction. Solo recording is powerful because it is private, available, and easy to repeat. But it cannot fully train turn-taking, clarification, or another person’s surprise.

**Nora:** The partner does not have to be a teacher.

**Eli:** No. A supportive partner can ask one follow-up question, say what was unclear, and prompt one repair. An AI conversation can also create extra turns, but the learner should still check important corrections and avoid treating every AI suggestion as perfect.

**Nora:** What if Amir has no partner today?

**Eli:** Use a follow-up card. After the first answer, turn over a card with a new question: “Why?” “Can you give an example?” “Has this changed over time?” “What would someone else say?”

**Nora:** He cannot prepare the exact second answer, so the card creates a small piece of real conversation.

**Eli:** And when a word disappears, he practices repair instead of stopping. “I don’t know the exact word, but it is a place where…” Or, “Let me say that another way.”

**Nora:** That is a speaking skill too. Good speakers do not know every word at every moment. They know how to keep the meaning alive.

## 7. Fear, Mistakes, and the Confidence Trap

**Nora:** We have explained the method, but Amir may still avoid the record button. Why?

**Eli:** Because speaking is public, fast, and connected to identity. A mistake can feel like evidence that we are not intelligent, even when it is only evidence that we are learning a difficult skill.

**Nora:** A meta-analysis brought together ninety-seven reports, with almost twenty thousand learners. It found a moderate negative connection between language anxiety and achievement.

**Eli:** But a correlation does not tell us a simple story. Anxiety may reduce practice or make performance harder. Difficult experiences may also increase anxiety. The relationship can move in both directions.

**Nora:** So we should not say, “Remove anxiety, and fluency will appear.”

**Eli:** No. We make the cost of practice smaller. Record alone. Use a familiar topic. Speak for thirty seconds. Work with one supportive person. Correct only one target. Keep the recording private if you want.

**Nora:** There is also a difference between discomfort and danger. Feeling awkward for thirty seconds is uncomfortable. It is not a disaster.

**Eli:** Amir can create evidence: “I answered the question. The listener understood my main point. I repaired one sentence. The second attempt was easier.”

**Nora:** Confidence grows from that evidence.

**Eli:** And from a better definition of success. Not “I made zero mistakes.” Try: “I completed the message and improved one thing.”

**Nora:** Marianna Pascal made a popular argument in a TEDx talk: many learners speak as if every conversation is an English test. Their attention moves from the message to the judgment.

**Eli:** That idea matches Amir’s experience, but we should call it an explanation, not proof. Still, the question is useful: during this round, am I trying to communicate, or am I trying to look perfect?

**Nora:** If perfection enters the studio with its red pen—

**Eli:** We give it a chair and tell it to wait until the feedback round.

**Nora:** **[laughs]** It may not enjoy the rule.

**Eli:** It does not have to enjoy the program. It only has to stop interrupting.

## 8. The Twenty-Minute Speaking Loop

**Nora:** Let’s put everything into one routine. Twenty minutes, one real question, no full written script.

**Eli:** Minute one: choose the question and write three ideas. For example, “Should people work from home?” Your three ideas might be time, focus, and teamwork.

**Nora:** Minutes two through five: give a four-minute first answer. If you finish early, add an example. If you get stuck, use a repair phrase. Do not restart every time you make a mistake.

**Eli:** Minutes six through eight: listen back. Find one message gap and one language or pronunciation target.

**Nora:** A message gap could be, “My opinion was unclear.” A language target could be, “I kept saying ‘good’ because I could not retrieve ‘convenient.’” A pronunciation target could be one word stress that made the sentence difficult to understand.

**Eli:** Minutes nine through eleven: answer the same question again. Use one or two improved phrases. Keep the message clear.

**Nora:** Minutes twelve and thirteen: give the answer again in two minutes. Shorter time does not mean speaking wildly fast. Remove repetition and keep the important points.

**Eli:** Minutes fourteen through sixteen: transfer. Answer a related question, or ask a partner for a follow-up. “When is working from home a bad idea?” Now the content changes, but some language can travel.

**Nora:** Minutes seventeen through twenty: review. Save three useful phrases and one target for next time. Do not save twenty corrections.

**Eli:** Let’s hear the full loop in one sentence: plan three ideas, speak from memory, diagnose two things, repeat with support, shorten the answer, transfer to a new question, and save a small amount for tomorrow.

**Nora:** What should Amir measure?

**Eli:** First, message completion. Did he make the point?

**Nora:** Second, pauses. Did he pause at natural idea boundaries, or get stuck inside every phrase?

**Eli:** Third, retrieval. Could he use the saved phrases without reading?

**Nora:** Fourth, intelligibility. Could a listener understand the important words?

**Eli:** Fifth, repair. When something failed, could he explain it another way?

**Nora:** Words per minute can be one measure, but it is not the judge of the whole performance.

**Eli:** And compare your own recordings with your own recordings. Another speaker may have a different level, topic, accent, and natural pace.

## 9. A Seven-Day Test, Not a Seven-Day Miracle

**Nora:** Amir asked for a concrete plan, so here is a seven-day experiment.

**Eli:** On day one, answer a familiar question. Record, diagnose, and repeat.

**Nora:** On day two, return to the same question without reading the old answer. See what stayed available.

**Eli:** On day three, answer a related question and reuse three phrases.

**Nora:** On day four, choose a new topic but keep the same speaking loop.

**Eli:** On day five, work on one short pronunciation or rhythm target. Then use it immediately in an original answer.

**Nora:** On day six, add interaction. Speak with a partner, use an AI conversation carefully, or use follow-up cards.

**Eli:** On day seven, return to the first question. Do not read the old script. Compare the first and final recordings.

**Nora:** Ask: Is the message clearer? Are the pauses better placed? Can I retrieve more useful phrases? Can I repair a missing word? Does the second question feel less frightening?

**Eli:** Seven days will not make anyone perfectly fluent. But it is enough time to test whether the practice is training real speech instead of only collecting knowledge.

**Nora:** Amir, the English did not arrive ten minutes late because you are bad at languages. It arrived when the pressure was gone and your brain had time to build the message.

**Eli:** Our job is to make that process faster through small, repeated, meaningful speaking—not through shame, magic, or endless preparation.

**Nora:** Before we leave, here is tonight’s question for you: “What is one thing you would improve in your city, school, or workplace?”

**Eli:** Write three ideas. Record your first answer. Choose one target. Then try again.

**Nora:** If this program helped you, subscribe to Dear English Radio. You can also leave your first sentence in the comments. It does not need to be perfect. It only needs to begin.

**Eli:** I’m Eli.

**Nora:** And I’m Nora. Keep the meaning alive, and we will meet you at the station next time.
