faq:
- q: Why add up if mess already means disorder?
  a: Mess and mess up can overlap in translation, but they are not freely interchangeable in every sentence.
    Mess up is an established informal expression for spoiling something, doing it badly, or making a
    mistake. The whole expression and its grammar must be learned together; up does not automatically
    mean that the mistake is serious.
- q: Does up always mean more, complete, or good?
  a: No. Higher, more, active, visible, and complete are useful branches of a meaning map, not fixed translations
    or moral labels. The base verb and context choose which branch, if any, is useful.
- q: Why can use up mean finish a supply?
  a: A limited resource moves toward its endpoint until none remains. The completion branch of up helps
    make that boundary noticeable, while use supplies the action and the object supplies the limited resource.
- q: Why can break down mean both fail and analyze?
  a: One useful road is loss of an organized working state, as when a machine breaks down. Another is
    division into smaller parts, as when a teacher breaks an idea down. The grammar and object reveal
    which road the speaker means.
- q: What is the best way to learn UP and DOWN expressions?
  a: Start with the physical picture, learn a few recurring meaning branches, inspect the base verb and
    grammar, predict the likely meaning, and then verify the whole expression in a reliable learner dictionary.
    Keep conventional or idiomatic combinations as complete units.

## Words and phrases

**particle** — A small word such as up or down working with a verb.

**scale** — A set of levels on which something can increase or decrease.

**boundary** — A limit or endpoint where an event can naturally stop.

**conventional expression** — A combination speakers normally learn and use as a unit.

**mess up** — Informal: spoil something, do it badly, or make a mistake; not necessarily a serious one.

**finish up** — Complete the final part; task-completion use is especially common in American English.

**fill up** — Make or become full; fill up the tank means reach its capacity.

**use up** — Use all of a limited supply so nothing remains.

**come up** — Appear, happen, or become relevant.

**break down** — Stop functioning, lose emotional control, or divide a whole into parts.

**what's up?** — An informal idiom asking what is happening or wrong, or greeting someone.

**Eli:** The lower-scale picture may support memory, but the object gives the decisive clue: a device has a level; an offer can be accepted or rejected.

**Nora:** A useful memory picture treats strong emotion as high activation. Lower that activity and you may `calm down` or `cool down`. This explains a current grouping, not the proven history of either phrase.

**Nora:** A second memory picture links `down` with stopped operation. A machine can `break down` and fail. A computer can `shut down`, which only means it stops operating and may be normal or intentional. Communication can `break down`. This is a current memory link, not necessarily one history.

**Eli:** The entity or situation tells us what kind of function can fail. A car has mechanical function. A discussion has communication. A person has emotional control.

**Nora:** “She broke down and cried” means her ability to continue or control the emotion gave way. The person did not physically fall into pieces.

**Nora:** A nearby control branch appears in `pin down a date` or `pin down the cause`. A loose object can be held in one place. An uncertain date or explanation becomes fixed and exact instead of continuing to move.

**Eli:** Again, learn the real expression first. The picture supports it; the picture does not give us permission to attach `down` to any verb we like.

**Nora:** Some expressions are less transparent. `Write down` is best learned as a stored expression meaning put something in writing. A fixed-record picture can support memory, but we should not pretend every phrase has one perfect modern diagram.

**Nora:** Exactly. Predict first, then check real usage.

## 6. BREAK DOWN: Two Useful Roads Here

**Eli:** Let’s slow down for `break down`, because one phrase shows why context matters.

**Nora:** Road one is failure. “The car broke down.” A useful analysis combines `break`, or lost integrity, with a `down` picture of collapsed function. The whole expression means the machine no longer works.

**Eli:** “Communication broke down.” No engine is involved, but the system of exchanging meaning stopped working.

**Nora:** Road two is analysis. “Let’s break the problem down into three steps.” The whole does not fail. Someone intentionally divides it into smaller parts.

**Eli:** Grammar often helps. “The car broke down” is intransitive and does not name an agent. “We broke the problem down” is transitive: someone acts on an object.

**Nora:** In these examples, grammar helps. In other sentences, context may do more of the work, so this is not a universal test.

**Eli:** Listen for the shape in our examples. In the failure sentences, the subject experiences the breakdown: the car, system, or negotiation stops holding together. In the analysis sentence, someone acts on a whole and creates understandable parts.

**Eli:** Try these two. “The payment system broke down during lunch.”

**Nora:** Failure. The system stopped functioning.

**Eli:** “The accountant broke the total down by month.”

**Nora:** Analysis. The accountant divided one total into smaller parts.

**Eli:** Same phrase, very different repair bill.

**Nora:** One needs a technician. The other needs a spreadsheet.
